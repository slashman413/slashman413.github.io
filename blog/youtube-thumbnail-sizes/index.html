YouTube Thumbnail Size & Best Practices (2026)

Updated July 2026 · ~6 min read

The thumbnail is the single most important image on your channel. It's the first thing a viewer sees, it decides whether they click, and click-through rate feeds directly into how much YouTube promotes your video. Yet a lot of creators upload a blurry, wrongly-sized frame and wonder why nobody clicks. This guide covers the exact dimensions to export, the format and size limits to respect, and the design habits that separate a thumbnail people scroll past from one they can't ignore.

The correct size and ratio

Export your thumbnail at 1280 × 720 pixels. That's the resolution YouTube has recommended for years and it's what you should treat as the standard today.

Resolution1280 × 720 px (minimum width 640 px)
Aspect ratio16:9
File formatJPG, PNG, or (typically) WebP
File sizeKeep it under the platform's ~2 MB ceiling

The 16:9 aspect ratio is the one that matters most. It's the shape of the YouTube player and every thumbnail slot across the site and app, so anything that isn't 16:9 gets letterboxed with black bars or awkwardly cropped. 1280 × 720 hits 16:9 exactly, which is why it's the safe default. Going higher — say 1920 × 1080 — is fine as long as you stay in 16:9 and under the file-size limit; it just gets scaled down. Never go below 640 px wide or the image looks soft on large screens.

Format and file-size limits

YouTube accepts JPG, PNG and generally WebP. As a rule of thumb: use PNG when your thumbnail has crisp text and flat graphics (it keeps edges sharp), and JPG when it's photo-heavy and you need to shave off kilobytes. Whatever you pick, keep the file comfortably under the roughly 2 MB limit that's applied to custom thumbnails. If you're bumping against it, compress rather than shrink the dimensions — you want to keep 1280 × 720 while getting the bytes down.

Treat these numbers as current-era guidance rather than permanent guarantees. Platforms adjust upload rules over time, so if an export ever gets rejected, check the limit shown in YouTube Studio at that moment.

Design tips that lift click-through

  • One clear focal point. A single subject — a face, an object, a bold number — reads instantly. Cluttered thumbnails lose in a crowded feed.
  • Faces and emotion. A close-up expressing a clear emotion (surprise, joy, tension) reliably draws the eye more than a neutral wide shot.
  • High contrast. Make your subject pop off the background with contrasting colors, an outline, or a subtle drop shadow.
  • Minimal text. Three to five big words at most. The thumbnail is the hook, not the whole title — which already appears next to it.
  • Consistent branding. A recurring color, font or layout makes your videos recognizable at a glance in subscriptions and search.
  • Don't over-promise. Clickbait that the video doesn't deliver tanks watch time, and watch time matters more than the click.

Legibility on mobile

Most YouTube viewing happens on phones, where your gorgeous 1280 × 720 export is displayed at the size of a postage stamp. This is the test that matters: shrink your thumbnail to roughly 120 pixels wide and look at it. Can you still tell what the video is about? Is the text readable? If not, simplify. Keep important text and faces away from the corners — the bottom-right is covered by the video's duration stamp, and platform overlays can clip the edges. Big type, few words and a single dominant subject survive the shrink; fine detail and long sentences disappear.

A/B testing your thumbnails

Even experienced creators guess wrong about which thumbnail will win, so test instead of assuming. YouTube has a built-in "Test & Compare" feature that rotates up to three thumbnails on a live video and reports which drives the best results, so you can let real audience behavior pick the winner. If that isn't available to you, do it manually: publish with one thumbnail, give it enough impressions to be meaningful, then swap in an alternative and compare the click-through rate in YouTube Studio Analytics. Change one variable at a time — the background, the text, the facial expression — so you learn why one won, not just that it did. Over a few videos you'll build an intuition for what your specific audience clicks on.

FAQ

What is the ideal YouTube thumbnail size? 1280 × 720 pixels at a 16:9 aspect ratio, kept under about 2 MB.

Can I use a vertical or square image? You can upload one, but it will be letterboxed or cropped to 16:9, wasting space and looking unprofessional. Always design in 16:9.

PNG or JPG? PNG for sharp text and graphics; JPG for photo-heavy thumbnails where you need a smaller file.

Why does my thumbnail look blurry? Usually it was exported too small or over-compressed. Start from a full 1280 × 720 canvas and avoid upscaling a tiny image.

Do I need a custom thumbnail? Yes if you can — auto-generated frames rarely capture attention, and a deliberate custom thumbnail almost always outperforms them.

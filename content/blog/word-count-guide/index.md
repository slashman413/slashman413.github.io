<p>"How many words should this be?" is one of the most common questions in writing — and it comes up for essays, blog posts, meta descriptions, product pages and social posts alike. The honest answer is "as many as the job needs, and no more," but that's not very useful when you're staring at a submission box with a hard limit.   <h2>Why length matters (and when it doesn't)</h2>
  <p>Length matters for three reasons: <strong>hard limits</strong> (a form rejects anything over 650 words, a meta description gets truncated in search results), <strong>reader attention</strong> (people scan and bounce, so every extra paragraph is a small cost), and <strong>context</strong> (a how-to needs enough depth to actually solve the problem). What length almost never does is help on its own. Padding a 600-word answer to 1,500 words to "look thorough" usually makes it worse — the reader has to wade through filler to find the substance. Treat the numbers below as targets that keep you honest, not quotas to fill.</p>

  <h2>Reading time, and the math behind it</h2>
  <p>Most adults read prose at about <strong>200–250 words per minute</strong> (silent reading of general-audience text); technical or dense material runs slower, around 150–200 wpm. To estimate reading time, divide your word count by your assumed speed:</p>
  <ul>
    <li>500 words ÷ 225 wpm ≈ <strong>2.2 minutes</strong></li>
    <li>1,000 words ÷ 225 wpm ≈ <strong>4.4 minutes</strong></li>
    <li>2,000 words ÷ 225 wpm ≈ <strong>8.9 minutes</strong></li>
  </ul>
  <p>Showing an estimated read time ("6 min read") sets expectations up front and tends to improve completion rates, because readers can decide to commit rather than abandoning halfway. Our counter calculates this for you as you type.</p>

  

  <h2>Words vs. characters: know which one you're limited by</h2>
  <p>Some limits are measured in <strong>words</strong> (essays, articles, assignments), others in <strong>characters</strong> (meta descriptions, tweets, bios, SMS, ad copy). The two don't convert cleanly. In English, a word averages about five letters plus a space, so a rough rule is <strong>one word ≈ 6 characters</strong> — meaning a 160-character meta description is only about 25–27 words. Characters almost always include spaces (and often punctuation and emoji, where a single emoji can count as two or more characters). When you're writing to a character budget, paste into a counter that shows both counts live so you don't discover the overflow after hitting submit.</p>

  <h2>How to hit a target without padding</h2>
  <ul>
    <li><strong>Write first, trim second.</strong> Get every idea down in a messy draft, then cut to the target. Editing down almost always produces tighter writing than padding up.</li>
    <li><strong>Cut the weakest 10% first.</strong> Redundant intros, throat-clearing ("In today's fast-paced world…"), and sentences that restate the previous one are the easiest words to lose with zero information cost.</li>
    <li><strong>To add length honestly,</strong> add an example, a counterpoint, a short how-to, or a specific number — not adjectives. New information earns its space; decoration doesn't.</li>
    <li><strong>For SEO,</strong> aim to fully answer the query and its obvious follow-ups. Length is a byproduct of completeness, not a goal. A focused 900-word answer can outrank a bloated 2,500-word one.</li>
    <li><strong>Keep drafts private</strong> by counting locally instead of pasting sensitive text into a site that uploads it to a server.</li>
  </ul>

  

  <h2>FAQ</h2>
  <p><strong>How long should a blog post be for SEO?</strong> Often 1,000–2,000 words, but the right length is whatever fully and concisely answers the reader's question. Depth and structure matter more than raw word count.</p>
  <p><strong>How is reading time calculated?</strong> Word count divided by an average reading speed of roughly 200–250 words per minute. Slower for technical material.</p>
  <p><strong>Do spaces count as characters?</strong> Usually yes. Most platforms and word processors include spaces (and punctuation) in the character count, so check whether a limit is "with spaces" or "without."</p>
  <p><strong>How many words is 160 characters?</strong> About 25–27 English words, using the rough estimate of six characters per word including the trailing space.</p>
